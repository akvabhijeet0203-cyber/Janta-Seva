01 — Inspiration

The Spark

It started with a photograph — a burst water main flooding a street in the early hours, posted on a neighborhood chat group. The image circulated for six hours. Residents tagged local officials. Officials asked residents to "please use the official portal." The portal required a category selection. Nobody could agree whether a burst pipe was a water issue or a roads issue. The complaint sat, uncategorized, in a digital limbo.

The system failed not because people didn't care — it failed because the bridge between a citizen's lived experience and the right department desk was never built. — the problem we set out to solve

We kept returning to a single question: why does a human being need to manually read thousands of complaints, guess their urgency, and forward them to the right team? Every minute spent triaging is a minute not spent fixing. In dense cities where water boards, electricity companies, road authorities, and sanitation departments each guard their own inbox, a complaint can bounce for days before anyone acts. We wanted to build that missing bridge — fast, accurate, and invisible to the citizen filing it.

The inspiration wasn't purely technical. It was the quiet frustration of a grandmother photographing a cracked footpath outside her building for the third time in a year, uncertain whether her complaint had ever been read. That image stayed with us through every sprint.

02 — Architecture

How We Built It

The system accepts complaints as text, images, or both. It passes each submission through a lightweight two-branch pipeline that runs in parallel before a fusion layer merges the signals and produces a department classification plus a priority score.

📝
Citizen Input (text / image)
🔤
NLP Branch (TF-IDF + intent)
🖼️
Vision Branch (CNN classifier)
⚖️
Fusion Layer (weighted merge)
🏷️
Department + Priority Output

The NLP branch tokenizes the complaint text, applies a TF-IDF vectorizer trained on a labeled corpus of civic complaints (water, electricity, roads, sanitation), and passes the result into a logistic regression classifier. Keyword signals like "pipe burst," "blackout," "pothole," or "garbage overflow" anchor the classification before any contextual layer runs. For ambiguous complaints, a lightweight BERT-based intent detector resolves department overlap.

The vision branch accepts an attached photograph and runs it through a fine-tuned MobileNetV2 model, chosen specifically for its inference speed on CPU. The model was fine-tuned on scraped images of civic infrastructure defects across four classes matching our department taxonomy. A confidence threshold gate (τ = 0.62) ensures low-confidence image signals don't override strong text signals.

The fusion layer applies a weighted combination. When both signals are present, text confidence and image confidence are merged using a learned mixing weight α. When only one modality is submitted, the system falls back to that branch alone — no complaint is ever rejected for being incomplete.

The final department label is: argmax over the fused class probabilities. Priority P maps to three tiers — Critical (> 0.75), High (0.45–0.75), and Standard (< 0.45) — which route to different SLA queues in the backend.

04 — Challenges

What We Fought

Building a prototype in a constrained timeline meant confronting real problems fast. Here are the four that defined our development arc:

Challenge

Ambiguous complaints span multiple departments — a flooded road is both water and roads.

Solution

Introduced a multi-label soft-assignment step; primary department is the top-1 class, secondary is flagged if confidence is within 0.15 of primary.

Challenge

Labeled civic image datasets are almost nonexistent for Indian infrastructure contexts.

Solution

Scraped, manually tagged, and augmented a 2,400-image dataset using rotation, blur, and brightness jitter to simulate field photo conditions.

Challenge

Citizens write complaints in mixed-language (Hinglish) text that breaks standard tokenizers.

Solution

Integrated IndicNLP preprocessing with a transliteration fallback, preserving keyword signals even when formal Hindi script is absent.

Challenge

Real-time latency — the vision branch added 800 ms on CPU, too slow for a live UX.

Solution

Switched from ResNet50 to MobileNetV2, applied quantization (INT8), and moved image preprocessing to async; total pipeline now runs in under 300 ms.

05 — Lessons

What We Learned

  • Data quality beats model complexity. Early iterations used a larger BERT model with a messy dataset and performed worse than a logistic regression trained on a carefully cleaned one. Garbage in, garbage out is not a cliché — it's a hard constraint.
  • Context is a design decision, not a technical one. Deciding what "urgency" means — whose definition, which lexicon, which thresholds — required conversations with people who understand civic governance, not just machine learning. The most important meetings we had were with a retired municipal engineer.
  • Multi-modal fusion is not addition. Simply concatenating text and image features made things worse. We learned that modalities must be weighted by their relative reliability per input, not combined naively. Trust the signal that is more confident, not the one that arrived first.
  • Latency is a civic equity issue. A system that takes three seconds to respond feels broken on a 2G connection. Optimizing for inference speed wasn't a nice-to-have — it was a prerequisite for this to be usable by the citizens who need it most.
  • Explainability matters more than accuracy. A 91% accurate black-box will not be adopted by a government department. Building a simple keyword-highlight explanation of why a complaint was classified a certain way took two extra days and made the system ten times more trustworthy in user testing.
· · ·

The prototype we built is imperfect — the dataset is small, the fusion weights are hand-tuned rather than learned end-to-end, and we have not yet tested at scale. But it demonstrates something real: the gap between a citizen filing a complaint and the right person reading it can be closed by a few hundred milliseconds of intelligent computation. That gap, multiplied across millions of complaints per year, is where civic trust is quietly eroded. We wanted to start closing it.

Smarter Cities Begin With Listening.

Every complaint is a citizen reaching out. This system ensures they are always heard — by the right person, at the right priority, in real time.
